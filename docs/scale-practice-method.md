# Violin Scale Practice Method

A structured scale practice routine for adult violin learners with approximately 3 years of experience. Covers 1st through 3rd position, with progressive key introduction, shift exercises, and arpeggios.

---

## Key Progression Method

The key progression follows **Carl Flesch's** circle-of-fifths ordering, which is the standard in violin pedagogy. Keys are introduced by adding one sharp or flat at a time, alternating between major and its relative minor. This approach builds finger patterns incrementally — each new key changes only one finger placement from the previous.

The progression is divided into three skill levels based on technical demand (number of accidentals, string crossings, and position requirements).

### Why circle of fifths?

Flesch (*Scale System*, 1926) and Galamian (*Principles of Violin Playing and Teaching*, 1962) both use the circle of fifths as the organising principle. Each new key adds exactly one new accidental, so the left hand adapts gradually. This is preferred over chromatic ordering (C, C#, D, D#...) which jumps unpredictably between finger patterns.

## Position Work

### 1st Position

The home position. All four fingers are placed in their natural spacing:
- 1st finger: one whole step above the open string
- 2nd finger: adjusts between high (whole step above 1st) and low (half step above 1st) depending on key
- 3rd finger: typically a 4th above the open string
- 4th finger: one octave above the next lower open string

**When it is mastered:** the player can play all Level 1 scales in tune with consistent tone.

### 2nd Position

The hand shifts up one step so that 1st finger sits where 2nd finger was in 1st position.

**Introduction (Galamian method):** begin with scales the student already knows in 1st position. Play the same scale but start the shift after the first three notes. This lets the ear check intonation against the known 1st-position version.

**Key exercise:** play a one-octave G major scale starting on the G string. Play G-A-B in 1st position, then shift to 2nd position on the C and continue C-D-E-F#-G. Reverse descending.

### 3rd Position

The hand shifts up so that 1st finger sits where 3rd finger was in 1st position (a minor 3rd / major 3rd above the open string, depending on the key).

**Introduction:** most method books (Suzuki Book 4–5, Sassmannshaus Vol. 3) introduce 3rd position via the A string, where 1st finger in 3rd position plays C# or C natural — notes easily checked against the open A string (a 3rd above).

## Shift Practice

### General Principles (from Galamian)

1. **Anticipate the shift mentally** — hear the target note before moving.
2. **Release finger pressure** during the shift — the hand glides, it does not jump.
3. **Guide finger technique** — the finger that is currently down guides the hand to the new position; the target finger drops after arriving.
4. **Practice slowly with an audible slide** first, then gradually eliminate the slide.
5. **Check intonation** by playing the target note as a harmonic or against an open string.

### Shift Exercises

#### Exercise 1: 1st → 3rd position slide (A string)

```
1st pos: A(open) - B(1) - C#(2) - [shift] → 3rd pos: D(1) - E(2) - F#(3) - G#(4) - A(4 stretch or shift)
```

- Shift on 1st finger: 2nd finger (C#) guides the slide up to D (1st finger in 3rd pos)
- Practice the shift note pair (C#→D) in isolation, with a slow glissando
- Then integrate into the full scale

#### Exercise 2: 1st → 2nd position slide (A string)

```
1st pos: A(open) - B(1) - [shift] → 2nd pos: C#(2) - D(2) - E(3) - F#(4)
```

- Shift from 1st to 2nd position using 1st finger as guide
- 1st finger slides from B up to C (or C#)
- Smaller shift — requires precise intonation control

#### Exercise 3: 2nd → 3rd position slide (E string)

```
2nd pos: F#(1) - G#(2) - [shift] → 3rd pos: A(1) - B(2) - C#(3)
```

- Guide with 1st or 2nd finger depending on context
- Check arrival note against open A string

#### Exercise 4: Descending shifts (all positions)

Descending shifts are harder because gravity and arm weight work against you. Practice:
- Releasing finger pressure before shifting down
- Maintaining arm support (no collapsing elbow)
- Same slow-glissando approach, checking open strings for intonation

### Shift Practice Routine

For each scale that includes shifts:
1. Play the shift pair (last note before shift + first note after) 5 times slowly with audible slide
2. Play the shift pair 5 times at tempo with minimal slide
3. Play the full scale slowly, pausing briefly at the shift point
4. Play the full scale at tempo

## Arpeggios

Each scale should be paired with its tonic arpeggio (broken chord). Arpeggios reinforce the key feeling and train wider intervals.

### Arpeggio Patterns

For each key/mode in the practice set, play:

1. **Tonic triad arpeggio** — root, 3rd, 5th, octave, and back down
   - Major keys (ionian): major triad (root, major 3rd, perfect 5th)
   - Minor keys (aeolian): minor triad (root, minor 3rd, perfect 5th)

2. **Extended arpeggio (Level 2+)** — root, 3rd, 5th, octave, continue to 10th if range permits

### Arpeggio in Each Level

- **Level 1:** one-octave tonic triad arpeggio in 1st position, quarter notes
- **Level 2:** two-octave tonic triad arpeggio, 1st–3rd position, with shift
- **Level 3:** two-octave tonic triad arpeggio, all three positions, eighth notes

### Example: G Major Arpeggio

```
Level 1 (1st pos): G - B - D - G (one octave, quarter notes)
Level 2 (1st→3rd): G - B - D - G - B - D - G (two octaves, shift at D)
Level 3 (1st→2nd→3rd): same notes, shift at B (1st→2nd) and at G (2nd→3rd)
```

## Sources

1. **Flesch, Carl.** *Scale System.* Carl Fischer, 1926 (revised edition 1987). The standard reference for systematic scale practice. Organises scales by circle of fifths with progressive technical demands. Used as the primary key progression source.

2. **Galamian, Ivan.** *Principles of Violin Playing and Teaching.* Prentice-Hall, 1962 (3rd edition, Shar Products, 1985). Chapter on shifting technique: guide finger method, mental preparation, release of pressure. Used as the primary source for shift practice principles.

3. **Galamian, Ivan & Neumann, Frederick.** *Contemporary Violin Technique, Vol. 1: Scale and Arpeggio Exercises.* Galaxy Music, 1962. Companion exercise book to *Principles*. Provides specific shift exercises and arpeggio patterns in all positions.

4. **Sassmannshaus, Kurt.** *Early Start on the Violin, Vols. 1–4.* Baerenreiter, 2008. Progressive method introducing positions: 1st position (Vols. 1–2), 3rd position (Vol. 3), 2nd and higher positions (Vol. 4). Used for position introduction sequencing.

5. **Suzuki, Shinichi.** *Suzuki Violin School, Vols. 1–8.* Summy-Birchard / Alfred Music. 3rd position introduced in Book 4–5. While primarily a repertoire method, the progressive key introduction aligns with the circle-of-fifths approach used here.

6. **Fischer, Simon.** *Practice: 250 Step-by-Step Practice Methods for the Violin.* Edition Peters, 2004. Practical guidance on shift practice, arpeggio practice, and scale routine structure. Recommended for supplementary exercises.
